01 — Introduction

Introduction

The Gay Asians of Toronto (GAT) was founded in 1980 after Richard Fung's return from the 1979 National March on Washington for Lesbian and Gay Rights.1 Inspired by the march and by Gerald Chan's 1980 essay "Out of the Shadows," Fung reached out to Chan and began circulating word of a gay Asian community forming in Toronto.

1Amin Ghaziani, The Dividends of Dissent: How Conflict and Culture Work in Lesbian and Gay Marches on Washington (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2008), 64–65.

Word travelled through The Asianadian, a Toronto-based Asian Canadian magazine, and through a discussion group that met at 519 Church. That discussion group became the Gay Asians of Toronto.

GAT was founded with two intentions at once: to organize politically around the experience of being both queer and Asian in Toronto, and to build community around queer Asian diasporic life. It was the first queer group in Canada organized explicitly along racialized lines.2 The 1982 documentation of the first AIDS case in Toronto shifted the organization's focus, not just within Canada but across North America, toward AIDS prevention — a shift most visible in GAT's 1989 grant application to the Toronto Board of Health, which created the Gay Asian AIDS Project (GAAP).

2While GAT was distinctive for organizing along racialized lines, other queer Toronto groups of the era — The Body Politic, GATE, LOOT — were active earlier, in the 1970s.

GAT disbanded in the early 2000s. Asian Canadian AIDS Services (ACAS), a direct offshoot of GAAP, is still operating as of 2025.

GAT was far from the first or only queer political organization in the city — but it was the first that organized explicitly along racialized lines, in a landscape of queer and Asian organizing that had, until then, been overwhelmingly white, male, and cisgender. GAT's activism eventually extended globally, becoming an instrumental part of queer political organizing in both Hong Kong and Japan.

02 — Origins

Origins

Two events converged to form GAT: Richard Fung's trip to the 1979 National March on Washington, and Gerald Chan's 1979 essay in The Asianadian. An estimated 75,000 to 125,000 people marched on Washington that October, and Fung came away with what he described as a model for organizing racialized solidarity within a broader queer movement.3

3Richard Fung, "Asians: Gay & Proud," The Asianadian, Winter 1979, 30, asianadian.ca/v2n3.

Chan's essay was among the first published works in Canada to discuss racialization and queerness together. It argued that the near-total absence of Asian gay men and lesbians from public queer life was a form of double repression — invisible within white queer spaces, and unrecognized within Asian community life.

It is apparent that there is a great need for help among Asiandian gays and lesbians out there. But such help should ideally come not from our white brothers and sisters, but from those who are facing the problems themselves. Gerald Chan, "Out of the Shadows," 1979

Gaining access to Asian Canadian spaces brought its own friction. Queer Toronto in the 1980s was more visible than it had been a decade earlier, but the city's major queer institutions — The Body Politic, the University of Toronto Homophile Association — remained overwhelmingly white, cisgender, male, and middle-class.4 Richard Fung's 1984 article "Gay Asians of Toronto" named this directly: outreach efforts at the University of Toronto's Chinese Students Association had been met with the assumption, among white gay organizers, that non-white and non-gay were mutually exclusive categories.

4Tom Warner, Never Going Back: A History of Queer Activism in Canada (Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 2002), 59.

03 — Activities and events

Activities and events

Celebrasian

GAT's activity through the 1980s and 1990s is best documented through its quarterly newsletter, Celebrasian, which carried directories of allied queer Asian organizations across Canada and internationally — Chicago, New York, Houston, Hong Kong, Manila, London. Beginning in 1983, GAT held an annual celebration under the same name, drawing panels, theatre, and workshops, with attendees from organizations as far afield as AMALGM in Boston.5

5S. H. Chua, "Asian Lesbian/Gay Conference in Toronto," AMALGM Newsletter, March 1988, 10–12.

The Gay Asian AIDS Project (GAAP)

GAT's activism in Toronto increasingly centred on AIDS prevention. In 1989 the organization secured a grant from the Toronto Board of Health, forming the Gay Asian AIDS Project. GAAP produced pamphlets and outreach programming aimed at a pan-Asian audience, publishing materials in Vietnamese, Cantonese, Mandarin, and Tagalog to reach non-English-speaking communities.6

6GAT AIDS Prevention Pamphlets, 1990, Gay Asians of Toronto Fonds, Box 65, Folder 62-07-046, the ArQuives, Toronto.

A renewal grant was requested in early 1990; whether it was approved is not recorded in the surviving files.

Controversy in The Body Politic

Not all of GAT's engagement with Toronto's broader queer press was smooth. In an April 1985 letter to the editor, Alan Li criticized The Body Politic for centring the concerns of middle-class white gay men while treating racism and class as secondary issues — a controversy over an ad in the same magazine that resurfaced, and was still being discussed, at a 2016 University of Toronto symposium.7

7Michael Connors Jackman, "Receptions of the Past: Commemoration, Racism, Desire," Somatechnics 7, no. 2 (2017): 293–295.

04 — Transnational connections

Transnational connections and collaborations

Toronto was GAT's home base, but its outlook was never purely local. The organization's 1985 hosting of the International Gay Association Conference connected GAT members with counterparts from across Canada and abroad, and those contacts shaped years of collaboration that followed — most closely with organizations in Vancouver, Boston, Japan, and Hong Kong.

Vancouver

GAT's first documented contact with Vancouver appears in a September 1984 Celebrasian item congratulating the newly formed Gay Asians of Vancouver Area (GAVA). GAVA's newsletter, first named Voices and later renamed Orientasian, frequently cited GAT as a model — and in 1991, GAVA requested GAT and GAAP's AIDS-prevention materials for a parallel initiative on the coast.8

8Alan Li, "Letter from Dr. Alan Li to Tom Szeto," Voices, June 1991, 11.

Hong Kong

GAT's influence reached Hong Kong through Alan Li, a GAT member who helped initiate the 10% Club, a local gay activist group formed two years after the planned 1988 Asian Gay Conference in Hong Kong. The 10% Club produced plays and social events and drew strong initial response, though its activity declined after Li returned to Canada in 1988.9

9Jack Lo, "Gay Hong Kong: A Report," AMALGM Newsletter, November 1, 1988, 15.

05 — Conclusion

Conclusion

GAT's work was ambitious and far-reaching, grounded in shared ideals of community- and solidarity-building at both the local and transnational level. Its collaborations extended from Vancouver and Boston to Japan and Hong Kong, where the 1986 Gay Asian Conference and the formation of the 10% Club stand as its clearest transnational legacies.

Twenty-first century queer organizing in Toronto did not form in a vacuum. Pride Toronto — today the largest pride festival in Canada and the second largest in the world — states that its organizing body values intersectionality, accessibility, and anti-racism.10 That vocabulary has roots in work GAT began decades earlier: only through recognizing that history can the realities of today's activism be fully understood.

10"About Us," Pride Toronto, accessed August 14, 2025, pridetoronto.com.
